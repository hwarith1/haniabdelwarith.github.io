Welcome

I study state‑formation in developing societies. In my ongoing and completed research I show that postcolonial states supply education and mosques unevenly and strategically as part of broad logics of social control. These processes leave behind manufactured meritocracy, territorialized marginalization and enduring political divisions in nations under nondemocratic rule.
My research draws on and advances regional expertise in the Middle East and North Africa, and a broad archival and historical engagement with the postcolonial world to study the relationship between contention, educational supply, status, and cleavage construction across those societies that decolonized after the Second World War.
Current and published research projects have explored macro-level processes in Tunisia, Egypt, Algeria, and Turkey. I have published co‑authored work with H.E. Ceyhun on the relationship between political polarization and religious identification in Turkey Mediterranean Politics.
In my dissertation, Fellahin into Frenchmen: Social Control and Educational Supply in the Postcolonial Maghreb, I demonstrate that leaders in Tunisia and Morocco withheld secondary education from regions linked to colonial‑era rebellions after independence.
I am grateful to be advised by Amaney Jamal, Mark Beissinger, and Elizabeth Nugent.
